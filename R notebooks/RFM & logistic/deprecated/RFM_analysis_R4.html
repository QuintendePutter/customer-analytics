RFM Analysis

Introduction

RFM, recency (R), frequency (F) and monetary value (M) are the most often used database marketing metrics used to quantify customer transaction history. RFM analysis segments customer into groups according to these measures and relates these segments to the likelihood of responding to a marketing offer. This notebook discusses the measures, segmentation, usefulness for guiding marketing decisions, and extensions to the basic model.

Let’s create a numeric 0/1 variable for respmail and mail, which are factors.

ebeer$respmail.num<-as.numeric(ebeer$respmail)
ebeer$mailing.num<-as.numeric(ebeer$mailing)

The probability of observing \(x\) people respond out of \(n\) people mailed is described by a binomial distribution. \[P(x|n, p) = {n \choose x} p^{x} (1-p)^{n-x}\] It has one paramter, \(p\), the probability of response. If we fit the model to the data, the maximum likelihood estimate is of \(p\) is \[\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\] What is \(x\), \(n\) and \(\hat{p}\) in our data? \(x\) is the number of responses, 616. \(n\) is the total number of people mailed, 4952. So therefore \(\hat{p}\), the overall response rate (probability of response):

p_hat<-sum(ebeer$respmail.num[ebeer$mailing.num==1])/sum(table(ebeer$respmail))
p_hat
## [1] 0.124

The standard error of the proportion is \(\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\), and the 95% confidence interval is calculated as follows.

n = sum(ebeer$mailing.num==1)
alpha = .05
z = qnorm(1-alpha/2)
p_hat + c(-1,1)*z*sqrt(p_hat*(1-p_hat)/n)
## [1] 0.115 0.134

Creating R, F and M segments separately.

Let’s first create segments from each variable separately; we build our own quantile function. We create separate segments for R, F, and M. We do this by creating quantiles, divide into 20% most recent, 20% next most recent, etc.

ntiles <- function(x, bins) {
  quantiles = seq(from=0, to = 1, length.out=bins+1)
  cut(ecdf(x)(x),breaks=quantiles, labels=F)
}

Full RFM analysis

Now do the full RFM analysis. Remember, the idea is that

  1. We first sort by R, create segments. (we already did this.)
  2. Within each R segment, we sort F and create RF segments.
  3. Within each RF segment, we sort M and create RFM segments.

First, we change ebeer into data.table Within each R group, we create F groups -> RF groups. Within each RF group, we create M groups -> RFM groups

dt = data.table(ebeer)
nbins = 5
dt[, RFgroup := paste0(as.character(Rgroup), as.character(ntiles(F, bins = nbins))), by = c('Rgroup')]
dt[, RFMgroup := paste0(as.character(RFgroup), as.character(ntiles(M, bins = nbins))), by = c('RFgroup')]

# put it back to data.frame
ebeer = data.frame(dt)

# change it to a factor variable
ebeer$RFMgroup<-as.factor(ebeer$RFMgroup)

Shrinking the estimates

In RFM analysis, we fit a separate binomial model to every segment in the data. Our estimates of the segment specific response rates are \[\hat{p}_s = \frac{x_s}{n_s}\] here \(x_s\) is the number of responses in segment \(s\) (6 for segment 90), \(n_s\) is the number of people mailed (16 for segment 90).

Right now we assume that these segments response rates are entirely independent of each other. In other words, knowing what the overall response rate is tells us nothing about e.g., segment 90.

But if we make an assumption about the distribution of response rates across segments, we could use that common distribution to “borrow” information from the other segments. Below is the distribution of response rates. The red curve is a beta distribution, the distribution we will be using to describe how the response rates vary over segments.

par(mai=c(.9,.8,.2,.2))

why use a beta distribution instead of e.g., a normal distribution?

We will fit the distribution of rates to the actual data. So our assumption here is really just the form of the distribution. The parameters of the distribution will be estimated directly from the data. (This is called Empirical Bayes estimation.)

More formally, we assume that \(p_s\) comes from a common beta distribution where \(a\) and \(b\) are parameters we fit to the data (just as \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are parameters you fit in the normal distribution). \(B(a,b)\) is an integration constant and is not important. This is the beta distribution. \[ f(p | a,b) = \frac{p^{a-1} (1-p)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)}, \qquad a>0, b>0\] The mean of the beta distrtion is \(E[p|a,b]=\frac{a}{a+b}\). Here are some of the shapes the beta distribution can take on.

Now to get the model for responses and the distribution of response rates, we have to sum up all the possible response rates and probabilities of them occuring that would have given rise to the responses. In other words we have to integrate the binomial response given its response rate over its beta distribution. We get the resulting model, the Beta-binomial model: \[ \begin{array}{ccl} P(x_s|n_s, a, b)& = & \displaystyle \int P(x_s | p_s, n_s) \; f(p_s|a,b) \; dp_s \\ & = & \displaystyle {n_s \choose x_s} \frac{B(a+x_s,b+n_s-x_s)}{B(a,b)} \end{array} \]

Now that \(p_s\) has a distribution, we no longer have an estimate. We can talk about its posterior mean, after observing \(x_s\) responses for segment \(s\) in the model: \[ \begin{array}{ccl} E(p_s|x_s, n_s) & = & \displaystyle \frac{a+x_s}{a+b+n_s}\\ & = & \displaystyle \frac{x_s}{n_s} \left( \frac{n_s}{a+b+n_s} \right) + \frac{a}{a+b} \left( \frac{a+b}{a+b+n_s} \right) \end{array} \] This shows exactly how much we rely on the individual segment estimate \(\frac{x_s}{n_s}\) and the population response rate across segments \(\frac{a}{a+b}\). The expressions in parentheses are weights that sum to 1. The more people mailed in each segment, the higher the weight \(\left( \frac{n_s}{a+b+n_s} \right)\) relative to \(a+b\), and therefore the more emphasis placed on the segment-specific response rate \(\frac{x_s}{n_s}\). In that case there is not so much “shrinking to the mean.” On the other hand, if \(n_s\) is small relative to \(a+b\), the larger the weight \(\left( \frac{a+b}{a+b+n_s} \right)\), and the more emphasis placed on the population mean, \(\frac{a}{a+b}\).
